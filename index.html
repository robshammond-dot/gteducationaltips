Feedback That Earns Its Place

A short guide to the feedback worth bringing home from an educational, the feedback that wastes the trip, and how to tell the difference before you write a word.

1Why this matters

An educational is one of the most expensive things we do; flights, nights, host time, and selling days off the desk. The return on that cost is the feedback you bring back, and only one kind of feedback pays it off: the kind we can act on.

Useful feedback does one of two jobs. It either helps an agent sell the product with more authority, or it helps the office decide which suppliers to back and how to rank them. If a comment does neither, it is noise, however nicely it is written.

2The ten-second test

Before you write anything down, run it past three questions:

  1. Could an agent repeat this to a client and have it genuinely help the sale?
  2. Could the office do something with it; sell it harder, raise it with the supplier, or move the property up or down a tier?
  3. Could it only be said about this property, or could it be said about any lodge in Africa?

If the first two are "no" and the third is "any lodge", delete it and write something real instead.

3Feedback that wastes the trip and why

Empty adjectives. "Stunning", "beautiful", "world-class".
Why it fails: every property is beautiful, so this tells a client nothing and tells the office nothing. It cannot be sold, ranked, or fixed. When a client asks "why this lodge over the other one", "it was stunning" is not an answer.
Brochure language repeated back to us.
Why it fails: the whole reason we send you is to verify the brochure with your own eyes. Copying the marketing copy is the one thing we could already do from the desk; it adds nothing, and it quietly signals you did not look closely.
A score with no reason. "Seven out of ten."
Why it fails: a bare number cannot be acted on. We do not know what cost the three points, so we cannot raise it with the supplier, set client expectations, or compare it fairly against the next property.
Personal taste dressed as assessment. "I did not like the food."
Why it fails: your palate is not the product. The same plate only matters if you frame it for a client, for example "very rich and a set menu, so light eaters and fussy children may struggle".
Vague complaints with no anchor. "The wifi was bad", "service was slow".
Why it fails: with no where, when, or how much, the office cannot take it to the supplier and cannot weigh it against the rest of the stay. It reads as a mood, not a fact.
Logistics only, with nothing about the product. "The bus was late."
Why it fails: useful for our own operations, so note it separately, but it is not why you were there. The product still has to come home with you.

4Feedback that is gold and exactly what makes it so

A detail an agent can repeat word for word to a client.
What makes it gold: for example, "Suites one to four face the river and you hear hippos from your deck at night." It is a selling hook, it is specific to this property, and it survives the client's "says who", because the answer is "our consultant stood on that deck".
Hard facts and numbers.
What makes it gold: room count, exact star rating, transfer time from the airport, walk from the furthest room to the main area, vehicles on a game drive. Numbers are what let us match a property to the right client; an older traveller and a thirty-something honeymooner need different answers, and only facts give them.
Clear persona matching, in both directions.
What makes it gold: "Best for repeat-safari clients who want exclusivity; not ideal for first-timers who want the big herds, or young families." This is the single most valuable line you can write, because it directly prevents the mismatched booking that becomes a complaint later.
Honest comparison inside our own portfolio.
What makes it gold: "Stronger service than the comparable lodge, but the rooms are smaller and there is no pool." It positions the property for us; we sell a range, and we sell it best when we know where each one sits against the others.
A red flag with context and a fix.
What makes it gold: "Check-in took forty minutes for our group of nine with one host on duty; for family groups I would confirm arrival staffing in advance." It protects the client and the brand, and gives us something concrete to manage rather than a grudge.
The "one thing" line.
What makes it gold: the single sentence you would say to a client to make them want this property. If you can write that line, you understood the place well enough to sell it. If you cannot, you have not looked hard enough yet.

5From first pass to sellable

The same observation, written twice. The left is what usually comes back; the right is what we can actually use.

Example Lodge was stunning.
Example Lodge has eight suites, each with a private plunge pool facing the river, and its stretch of private frontage means almost no other vehicles on a drive. Best for honeymooners and repeat clients who want a marquee destination without the crowds.
The food was great.
Dinner is a set three-course menu with one nightly choice, not a buffet, and they handled a gluten-free request without fuss. Flag to clients who like variety, or who eat late, that the kitchen runs to a fixed service.
Service was a bit slow.
Front desk took forty minutes to check in our group of nine with one host. Fine for couples; for larger family bookings I would ask the lodge to confirm extra arrival staffing.Internal note, not for client-facing material, until it is resolved with the supplier.

6Look through the same four lenses every time

Use these four on every property so feedback stays comparable across the portfolio.

Room hard stats
Count, configuration, view, what works and what is dated, family and connecting options.
Safety and vibe
How it feels on arrival and after dark; lighting, walkways, who else is staying, the read you would give a nervous first-timer.
Standard of being
Does it hold up past the showpiece moment; at breakfast, on an ordinary afternoon, with the staff who are not the manager.
Persona matching
Who it is for, who it is not for, and the honest price-to-experience read.

7Share it, or shelve it

Not everything you bring back can be circulated. Sort each comment the moment you write it.

Safe to share

  • Selling hooks and the "one thing" line
  • Hard facts and numbers
  • Persona guidance, who it suits
  • Positive differentiators
  • Anything a client could read and trust

Internal only

  • Criticism that names individual staff
  • Commercial or pricing observations
  • Unverified rumour or hearsay
  • Any red flag still unresolved
  • Anything that would embarrass us if the supplier read it

The test: would I be comfortable if this exact wording reached the supplier, or a client, by accident? If not, it stays internal until it is reworded or resolved.

8Capture it on the spot

Fill these in at the property, before the next stop blurs it. Five minutes there beats an hour guessing later.

  • The one thing I would tell a client about this property is ............................................
  • Best suited to clients who ............................................
  • Not ideal for clients who ............................................
  • Hard facts: rooms ...... star rating ...... transfer time ...... view ............
  • Against our other property it is stronger on ............ and weaker on ............
  • Honest weak point (internal) ............................................
  • Would I send my own family here, and why? ............................................
